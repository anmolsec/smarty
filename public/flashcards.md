## Accounting
Q: What is the basic accounting equation?
A: Assets = Capital + Liabilities.
---
Q: What is a trial balance used for?
A: It checks the arithmetical equality of debit and credit balances before final accounts are prepared.
---
Q: When is a bank reconciliation statement prepared?
A: When the cash-book bank balance differs from the balance shown by the bank statement.
---
Q: What is the usual treatment of outstanding expenses?
A: Add the amount to the expense in the Statement of Profit and Loss and show it as a current liability.
---
Q: What is the usual treatment of prepaid expenses?
A: Deduct the amount from the expense and show it as a current asset.
---
Q: What does the matching concept require?
A: Match expenses with the revenue of the same accounting period.
---
Q: Why are working notes important in an accounting answer?
A: They make the method visible and can earn step marks even if the final balance is wrong.
---
Q: What is a capital expenditure?
A: Expenditure that creates or improves a long-term asset or gives benefit beyond the current period.
---
Q: What is revenue expenditure?
A: Ordinary recurring expenditure incurred to earn revenue in the current period.
---
Q: What is depreciation?
A: Systematic allocation of an asset's depreciable amount over its useful life.
---
Q: What is the WDV method?
A: Depreciation is charged on the opening written-down value of the asset each period.
---
Q: Where is closing stock shown when it is outside the trial balance?
A: On the credit side of Trading Account and as a current asset in the Balance Sheet.
---
Q: What is a suspense account?
A: A temporary account used to make a trial balance agree until one-sided errors are rectified.
---
Q: What is goodwill on admission of a partner compensating for?
A: The old partners' sacrifice of future profit share.
---
Q: What is the sacrificing ratio?
A: Old share minus new share of each old partner.
---
Q: What happens to a partner's share at retirement?
A: It is acquired by continuing partners in their gaining ratio unless the agreement states otherwise.
---
Q: What is forfeiture of shares?
A: Cancellation of shares when a shareholder fails to pay a call due, subject to the terms of issue.
---
Q: Can forfeited shares be reissued at a discount?
A: Yes, but the discount cannot exceed the amount forfeited on those shares.
---
Q: What is a bill of exchange?
A: A written unconditional order directing one person to pay a certain sum to another or their order.
---
Q: What is a consignment?
A: Sending goods to an agent for sale while ownership remains with the consignor until sale.
---

## Business Laws
Q: What does Section 10 of the Indian Contract Act broadly state?
A: Agreements are contracts when made by competent parties with free consent, lawful consideration, lawful object, and not expressly void.
---
Q: What is an offer?
A: A willingness to do or abstain from doing something with a view to obtaining another person's assent.
---
Q: What makes acceptance valid?
A: It must be absolute, unqualified, and communicated in the prescribed or usual manner.
---
Q: What is a counter-offer?
A: A reply that changes the terms of the original offer; it rejects the original offer.
---
Q: Who is competent to contract?
A: A person of majority age, of sound mind, and not disqualified by law.
---
Q: What is the legal status of a minor's agreement?
A: It is void ab initio.
---
Q: What is consideration?
A: Something done, abstained from, or promised at the desire of the promisor.
---
Q: What is free consent?
A: Consent not caused by coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, or mistake.
---
Q: What is coercion?
A: Committing or threatening to commit an act forbidden by criminal law, or unlawfully detaining property, to make someone agree.
---
Q: What is fraud?
A: A deliberate deception intended to induce another party to enter into a contract.
---
Q: What is a void agreement?
A: An agreement not enforceable by law.
---
Q: What is a contingent contract?
A: A contract to do or not do something if an uncertain collateral event happens or does not happen.
---
Q: What is breach of contract?
A: Failure or refusal to perform a contractual promise without lawful excuse.
---
Q: What is indemnity?
A: A promise to save another from loss caused by the promisor or another person.
---
Q: What is a guarantee?
A: A promise to perform the promise or discharge the liability of a third person in case of default.
---
Q: What is bailment?
A: Delivery of goods for a specific purpose on the understanding that they will be returned or dealt with as directed.
---
Q: What is a pledge?
A: Bailment of goods as security for payment of a debt or performance of a promise.
---
Q: What is a condition in a contract of sale?
A: A stipulation essential to the main purpose of the contract; its breach can allow repudiation.
---
Q: What is a warranty in a contract of sale?
A: A collateral stipulation; its breach generally gives a claim for damages, not repudiation.
---
Q: What must a law answer always end with?
A: A clear conclusion applying the legal rule to the given facts.
---

## Quantitative Aptitude
Q: What is simple interest?
A: Principal × Rate × Time ÷ 100.
---
Q: What is compound interest based on?
A: Interest is calculated on principal plus accumulated interest from earlier periods.
---
Q: What is an annuity?
A: A series of equal payments or receipts made at equal intervals.
---
Q: What is the nth term of an AP?
A: a + (n − 1)d.
---
Q: What is the sum of first n terms of an AP?
A: n/2 × [2a + (n − 1)d].
---
Q: What is the nth term of a GP?
A: ar^(n − 1).
---
Q: What does a probability of 0 mean?
A: The event is impossible.
---
Q: What does a probability of 1 mean?
A: The event is certain.
---
Q: What is P(A union B)?
A: P(A) + P(B) − P(A intersection B).
---
Q: What is conditional probability P(A given B)?
A: P(A intersection B) ÷ P(B), provided P(B) is not zero.
---
Q: What is the arithmetic mean?
A: Sum of observations divided by the number of observations.
---
Q: Which average is least affected by extreme values?
A: Median.
---
Q: What is the empirical relation among mean, median, and mode?
A: Mode = 3 Median − 2 Mean.
---
Q: What does standard deviation measure?
A: Dispersion of observations around their mean.
---
Q: What does a correlation coefficient of +1 indicate?
A: Perfect positive linear correlation.
---
Q: What does a correlation coefficient of 0 indicate?
A: No linear correlation.
---
Q: What is a quick MCQ rule when no path is clear?
A: Mark it for review and move on before it consumes the section's time budget.
---
Q: What is the approximate safe time limit for a difficult MCQ?
A: About 90 seconds before marking it for review.
---
Q: How many questions are in the Quantitative Aptitude paper?
A: 100 objective questions, with negative marking for incorrect answers.
---
Q: What should a formula sheet contain?
A: Only formulas and cues you repeatedly need, not a rewritten textbook.
---

## Business Economics
Q: What does the law of demand state?
A: Other things equal, quantity demanded falls when price rises and rises when price falls.
---
Q: What is price elasticity of demand?
A: The responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price.
---
Q: What is perfectly inelastic demand?
A: Quantity demanded does not change when price changes.
---
Q: What does the law of supply state?
A: Other things equal, quantity supplied rises with price and falls with price.
---
Q: What is equilibrium price?
A: The price at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.
---
Q: What is a production function?
A: A technical relationship between inputs used and output produced.
---
Q: What is fixed cost?
A: Cost that does not change with output in the short run.
---
Q: What is variable cost?
A: Cost that changes with output in the short run.
---
Q: What is marginal cost?
A: Addition to total cost from producing one more unit.
---
Q: What is perfect competition?
A: A market with many buyers and sellers, homogeneous product, free entry and exit, and price-taking firms.
---
Q: What is monopoly?
A: A market with a single seller and significant barriers to entry.
---
Q: What is national income?
A: The aggregate value of final goods and services produced by residents in an economy over a period.
---
Q: What is fiscal policy?
A: Government use of taxation, expenditure, and borrowing to influence the economy.
---
Q: What is monetary policy?
A: Central-bank actions to influence money supply, credit, and interest conditions.
---
Q: What is inflation?
A: A sustained increase in the general price level.
---
Q: What is balance of payments?
A: A systematic record of a country's economic transactions with the rest of the world.
---
Q: What is depreciation of a currency?
A: A fall in its market value relative to foreign currencies.
---
Q: What should you do if an economics graph is unclear?
A: Redraw it from memory, label axes, then check one explanation and retry a related question.
---
Q: What is the best order for an MCQ revision block?
A: Attempt, mark uncertainty, check the pattern of errors, and create one recall cue.
---
Q: What is the purpose of a mistake log?
A: To turn recurring errors into targeted future practice rather than repeat them blindly.

## Exam Strategy
Q: What is the first action before a timed paper?
A: Read instructions, check the duration, and decide the section order before solving.
---
Q: What is the purpose of a timer in practice?
A: It trains pacing and exposes where time is being lost.
---
Q: What should you do after a timed paper?
A: Review every miss and classify it before starting another paper.
---
Q: What does an error classified as concept gap require?
A: A short return to the underlying rule followed by a fresh adjacent question.
---
Q: What does an error classified as calculation require?
A: Slow down once, identify the step that failed, and repeat the calculation with a checking rule.
---
Q: What does an application error require?
A: Compare the facts to the rule and underline the fact that changes the answer.
---
Q: What does a time-management error require?
A: Adjust the time limit or question order for the next attempt.
---
Q: What is active recall?
A: Retrieving an answer from memory before looking at notes.
---
Q: Why should flashcards be short?
A: A short prompt tests one retrieval path and makes weak knowledge easier to identify.
---
Q: What should you do with a card answered confidently?
A: Space it out and spend the immediate review time on weaker cards.
---
Q: What should you do with a card answered incorrectly?
A: Read the answer, say it once in your own words, and see it again soon.
---
Q: What is progressive disclosure in a study plan?
A: Showing the current task first and keeping later workload out of immediate view.
---
Q: What is one useful end-of-session question?
A: What exact error or uncertainty will tomorrow's first block address?
---
Q: Why should a study session have a finish line?
A: A concrete finish line lowers avoidance and makes progress visible.
---
Q: What is a good recovery action after distraction?
A: Return to the smallest next action, such as one question or one working note.
---
Q: What is the role of breaks?
A: They reset attention; they should be planned rather than used to escape a difficult question.
---
Q: Why should you not open many resources at once?
A: Extra sources increase extraneous cognitive load and make the next action unclear.
---
Q: What is a useful daily target?
A: A measurable quantity of focused work, such as a chapter block, answer count, or timed question set.
---
Q: What should be reviewed at night?
A: Formulas, legal keywords, and the day's one or two most useful corrections.
---
Q: What should not be added in the final days before an exam?
A: Large new topics that displace recall, error correction, and sleep.
